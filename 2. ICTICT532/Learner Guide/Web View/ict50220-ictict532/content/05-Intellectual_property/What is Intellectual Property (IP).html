Intellectual property (IP) is the property of your mind or proprietary knowledge. It is a productive new idea you create. This can be an invention, trademark, design, brand or even the application of your idea.

Your idea must be something new or original, but determining whether your idea is new or not is not always easy. Applying for an IP right to protect your idea can be critical if you want to build a business and establish your presence in a market. As stated by Craig Venter, a geneticist and one of the first scientists to sequence the human genome, 'intellectual property is a key aspect for economic development.'

Different IP rights and copyright

Some forms of IP require formal application and examination before you can claim a right to ownership. Others do not.

  • Copyright is a separate form of intellectual property which does not require registration.
  • Copyright and circuit layout rights are administered by the Department of Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development and Communications and they assist the government in developing and managing copyright policy in the Copyright Act 1968 and Circuits Layouts Act 1989.
  • Business names, company names and domain names are not a type of IP.
  • IP Australia administer patents, trademarks, designs, and plant breeder's rights in Australia.

Before you apply for any IP rights, it is important to understand the differences between the four main rights and which one suits your idea.
