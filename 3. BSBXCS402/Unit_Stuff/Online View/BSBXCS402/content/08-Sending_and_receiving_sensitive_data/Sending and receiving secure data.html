Email security

The OAIC advises that 'email is not a secure form of communication' and that therefore procedures must be developed to 'manage the transmission of personal information via email'. In their guide to protecting personal information(this is an external link and opens in a new window), OAIC advises to:

  • avoid sending personal/sensitive information via unsecured email
  • use secured websites and online mailboxes to communicate information
  • use secure end-to-end encryption messaging if available
  • gain consent from recipients for emailing their personal information
  • reduce the risk of unauthorised, unintended disclosure by validating the authorised recipient's email address before sending unencrypted email
  • keep accurate records of external email traffic
  • send large amounts of personal information as password protected or encrypted attachments.

Passwords

Although passwords are a basic form of security for any system, they are also the easiest area to hack. Therefore, a strong password needs to be created. In this instance, 'strength' refers to the length of the password and the character combination of letters, numbers and symbols.

Weak passwords, such as dates, names and even the word 'password' are commonly used and these are also the first options attempted by hackers.

Strong passwords are longer and combine a more unpredictable mixture of characters. Passphrases are considered to be the stronger than passwords. This can involve using words that represent a nonsensical statement. aCorgimake5agreaTh@mburg3r! is an example of an extremely strong password.

Network security

Data security is not just about having encryption on the data during transmission, but also restricting the access to the location of the data. There are a number of areas where security to a system can be improved. Other security that needs to be implemented on a network:

  • a properly administered firewall
  • security suite that is industry recognised and gives end-point protection
  • two-factor authentication for accessing sensitive information or applications
  • good staff training in email protocol, especially in recognising phishing.
